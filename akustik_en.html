1. Origin: Everything jitters!

Whether you speak, clap your hands, or pluck a guitar – sounds always arise from one thing: movement! An object wobbles back and forth extremely fast. Physicists call this vibration. When your vocal cords vibrate in your throat, your voice is created. Place your hand on your larynx while speaking – you can feel the jittering!

📖 The Smart Dictionary: The Sound Source
The thing that jitters and creates the sound is called the sound source. It can be the skin of a drum, the string of a violin, or the metal prong of a tuning fork.

2. The Medium: The silent void of space

Imagine standing on the moon and a huge meteorite crashes silently into the ground. Silently? Yes! For the sound to travel from the tuning fork to your ear, it needs tiny "postmen". These are the air particles. The tuning fork pushes the air, the air particles push their neighbors, and so the sound wave travels to your ear.

📖 The Smart Dictionary: The Vacuum
Sound always needs a substance (a medium) to spread. This can be air, but also water or even iron! Where there is absolutely nothing – like in outer space – it is called a vacuum. Without air particles, there is no one to carry the sound. In space, no one can hear you scream!

3. The Oscilloscope: Pitch and Volume

Scientists want to see sound waves! For this, they use a microphone computer called an oscilloscope. It draws the invisible sound waves as jagged lines (wave crests and wave troughs) on a screen.

📖 The Smart Dictionary: Frequency & Amplitude
- Frequency (Pitch): How often does the wave vibrate per second? If it vibrates very fast (many narrow waves), the pitch is high like the squeak of a mouse. If it vibrates slowly, the pitch is low like the growl of a bear. This is measured in Hertz (Hz).
- Amplitude (Volume): How strongly does the wave swing out? If the wave crests are very high, the sound is loud. If they are tiny and flat, the sound is a quiet whisper.

4. Speed of Sound: The Big Race

Sound is fast, but light is the absolute flash champion! Light travels almost 8 times around the entire Earth in one second. Sound manages "only" 340 meters per second in air. That's why during a thunderstorm we always see the lightning first and only seconds later the thunder crashes in our ears!

📖 Calculating with the Thunder Trick:
Since sound takes about 3 seconds for 1 kilometer, you can calculate: Count the seconds between lightning and thunder. Divide the number by 3. The result is the distance of the storm in kilometers! (If you count 9 seconds: 9 ÷ 3 = 3 kilometers away).

5. The Echo: Sound Bounces!

Remember how light particles bounce off a mirror like bouncy balls? Sound waves do that too! If you shout loudly in the mountains, the sound waves fly off, bounce off the hard rock wall, and come back to you. We call this an echo (reflection of sound).

📖 Animals with Superpowers: Sonar
Bats hunt in the dark. They shout very high (ultrasound, so high that we humans cannot hear it). The sound bounces off a tiny mosquito and throws an echo back. The bat's ear calculates lightning-fast: Aha, the echo comes from the right and was there quickly – the mosquito is near! This is called echolocation (or sonar in submarines).

6. Resonance: When sounds destroy things!

Have you ever seen an opera singer shatter a glass with only her voice? That's no trick! Everything in the world has its own "favorite vibration" (natural frequency). If a sound hits exactly this frequency, the object suddenly starts to vibrate wildly. This sympathetic vibration is called resonance.

📖 The Guitar Box (Resonance Body)
A guitar string alone sounds very quiet. But it is stretched over a large wooden box (the guitar). The string forces the wood and the air in the box to vibrate in the same rhythm. This resonance body makes the sound loud and powerful!
