1. Origin: Everything Shakes!

Whether you're speaking, clapping your hands, or plucking a guitar – sounds are always created by one thing: Movement! An object wobbles back and forth very fast. Physicists call this vibration. When your vocal cords in your throat vibrate, your voice is created. Place your hand on your larynx while speaking – you can feel the shaking!

📖 Smart Dictionary: The Sound Source
The thing that shakes and creates the sound is called a sound source. It can be the skin of a drum, the string of a violin, or the metal of a tuning fork.

2. The Medium: The Silent Void of Space

Imagine standing on the moon and a huge meteorite crashes silently into the ground. Silently? Yes! For the sound from the tuning fork to reach your ear, it needs tiny "mailmen". These are the air particles. The tuning fork pushes the air, the air particles push their neighbors, and so the sound wave travels to your ear.

📖 Smart Dictionary: The Vacuum
Sound always needs a substance (a medium) to travel through. This can be air, but also water or even iron! Where there is absolutely nothing – like in space – it's called a vacuum. Without air particles, there's no one to carry the sound. In space, no one can hear you scream!

3. The Oscilloscope: Pitch and Volume

Scientists want to see sound waves! For this, they use a microphone-computer called an oscilloscope. It draws the invisible sound waves as jagged lines (wave crests and troughs) on a screen.

📖 Smart Dictionary: Frequency & Amplitude
- Frequency (Pitch): How often does the wave vibrate per second? If it vibrates very quickly (many narrow waves), the sound is high like a mouse's squeak. If it vibrates slowly, the sound is low like a bear's growl. This is measured in Hertz (Hz).
- Amplitude (Volume): How strongly does the wave swing out? If the wave crests are huge, the sound is loud. If they are tiny and flat, the sound is quiet and whispering.

4. Speed of Sound: The Great Race

Sound is fast, but light is the absolute flash champion! Light travels almost 8 times around the whole Earth in one second. Sound only manages "only" 340 meters per second in air. That's why we always see the lightning first during a thunderstorm and only hear the thunder roar in our ears seconds later!

📖 Calculating with the Thunder Trick:
Since sound takes about 3 seconds for 1 kilometer, you can calculate: Count the seconds between lightning and thunder. Divide the number by 3. The result is the distance of the storm in kilometers! (If you count 9 seconds: 9 ÷ 3 = 3 kilometers away).

5. The Echo: Sound Bounces!

Remember how light particles bounce off a mirror like bouncy balls? Sound waves do that too! When you shout loudly in the mountains, the sound waves fly off, hit the hard rock face, and come back to you. We call this an echo (reflection of sound).

📖 Animals with Superpowers: Sonar
Bats hunt in the dark. They shout very high (ultrasound, so high that we humans can't hear it). The sound bounces off a tiny mosquito and throws back an echo. The bat's ear calculates in a flash: Aha, the echo comes from the right and was there quickly – the mosquito is close! This is called echolocation (or sonar in submarines).

6. Resonance: When Sounds Destroy Things!

Have you ever seen an opera singer shatter a glass with only her voice? That's no trick! Everything in the world has its own "favorite vibration" (natural frequency). If a sound hits exactly this frequency, the object suddenly starts to vibrate wildly. This sympathetic vibration is called resonance.

📖 The Guitar Box (Resonance Body)
A guitar string alone sounds very quiet. But it is stretched over a large wooden box (the guitar). The string forces the wood and the air in the box to vibrate in the same rhythm. This resonance body makes the sound loud and powerful!
