1. The Invisible Particles (Charges)

Everything around us consists of tiny building blocks called atoms. Inside these atoms live even smaller particles that have an electrical charge. Imagine them as small, charged balls.

An atom: The heavy, positive nucleus in the center and the nimble, negative electrons around it.

  • 🔴 Protons: These are positively (+) charged. They are heavy, stay fixed in the nucleus, and almost never move.
  • 🔵 Electrons: These are negatively (-) charged. They are tiny and absolute whirlwinds. They zip around the nucleus in orbits!

The most important rule of electricity is:
Likes don't like each other (they repel). But opposites attract!

The basic rule: Like charges want to get away from each other, unlike charges want to be together.

2. What is Current Actually?

Imagine a river where water flows. Electric current is exactly the same – only instead of water, billions of tiny, negative electrons (-) flow.

So current is simply: moving electrons that all zip through a cable in the same direction.

Like water in a pipe: Many small electrons flow together through the cable.

For them to flow, they need a path (a cable). But watch out, not every material lets electrons through!

  • 🟢 Conductors: These are materials in which electrons can slide freely like on a slide. (Examples: copper, iron, gold, water).
  • 🔴 Insulators: These are "electron prisons." They hold the electrons tight; nothing can flow. (Examples: plastic, rubber, wood, glass).

3. Circuit & Resistance

For a lamp to light up, the electrons must be able to flow in a circle. They start at the battery, flow through the lamp, and must return to the battery. This is called a closed circuit.

A closed circuit: The current can flow in a circle.

The current brake (resistance): Even in a super conductor (like copper), electrons don't flow completely freely. The material slows them down a bit. A thin cable slows them down more than a thick cable. When electrons have to squeeze through a thin cable, they rub against each other and the cable gets hot. That's exactly how a toaster works!

4. The Battery: The Power Pump

Electrons are a bit lazy. They don't just flow on their own. They need a "push." This push is called voltage. A battery is like a pump that generates this push.

The interior of a battery: Chemistry provides the charge difference.

  • 🔴 The anode (negative pole): Here, chemical reactions create many free electrons. So it's extremely crowded here!
  • 🟢 The cathode (positive pole): Here, there is an electron shortage. The electrons really want to come over here.
  • 🔵 The electrolyte: This is a thick paste in the middle. It's like a wall! The electrons can't just fly directly across the inside. They must take the long detour outside through the cable (and through our lamp)!

5. The Capacitor: The Energy Storage

A capacitor stores energy, but differently than a battery. It works more like a stretched bow or a dam.

It consists of two metal plates that do not touch. In between is an insulator (e.g., air). When we pump electricity into it, electrons accumulate on one plate. They want to go to the other, positive plate, but the insulator blocks the way! The voltage increases.

A charged capacitor: The charges face each other, separated by the insulator.

The energy is now stored. The highlight: If you connect the capacitor to a flash lamp, for example, it can release all the stored energy lightning fast in one go!
