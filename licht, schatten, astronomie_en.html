🌑 Shadows in Space 🌍

Light always travels exactly straight ahead. By drawing the outermost light rays (marginal rays) past an obstacle, you can calculate exactly to the millimeter where a shadow falls!

1. Who Orbits Whom?

The sun radiates its light into space. As the Earth travels around the sun, the side that faces the sun is always bright. On the back, the Earth casts a shadow into the void (night side).

The light rays run straight from the sun to the Earth.

2. The Solar Eclipse (Construction)

The small moon moves in front of the huge sun. If we draw the light rays from the top and bottom edge of the sun past the moon, we see two areas: the deep black umbra (core shadow) and the gray penumbra (half shadow).

Where not a single ray of sunlight reaches, the eclipse is total (umbra).

Why is it suddenly dark during a total solar eclipse?

3. The Lunar Eclipse (Construction)

The Earth is much larger than the moon. The straight marginal rays of the sun form a huge, long umbra tube behind the Earth. The moon fits completely inside it!

The moon is completely immersed in the Earth's deep shadow.
