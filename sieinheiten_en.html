1. SI Units: The language everyone understands

Imagine you are building a treehouse with a friend in America. You say: "We need a board that is 5 feet long." Your friend measures with his feet – but they are much smaller than yours! The board doesn't fit. To avoid exactly this chaos, scientists all over the world have agreed on a common system: The International System of Units (SI).

📖 The Smart Dictionary: Measuring means Comparing!
When you measure, you always compare an object with a fixed standard. 1 meter is exactly the same length all over the world, whether in Germany, Japan or on the moon!

2. The 7 Base Quantities: The foundation of the world

Everything you can measure in the universe can be assembled from just 7 basic building blocks (base quantities). The most important three that you need almost every day are: Length (meter, m), Mass (kilogram, kg) and Time (seconds, s).

📖 The complete team of 7:
1. Length (meter) | 2. Mass (kilogram) | 3. Time (second)
4. Electric current (ampere) | 5. Temperature (kelvin)
6. Amount of substance (mole) | 7. Luminous intensity (candela)

3. Prefixes: From Kilo to Milli

Sometimes meters or grams are simply impractical. An ant is tiny, the distance to America is huge! For that we have "prefixes". They are like magic multipliers: Kilo always means "1000 times as much" (1 kilometer = 1000 meters). Milli means "one thousandth" (very tiny).

📖 The most important prefixes:
- Milli (m) = 1 / 1,000 (millimeter = tiny)
- Centi (c) = 1 / 100 (centimeter = small)
- Kilo (k) = 1,000 (kilogram = a lot)
- Mega (M) = 1,000,000 (megabyte = huge!)

4. Formulas: The secret recipes of physics

In physics we don't write long novels, we use formulas! They are like a short cooking recipe that shows how things are connected. Let's take speed. It depends on how much distance you cover in a certain time. The formula is: v = s / t (speed = distance divided by time).

📖 The Formula ABC:
Scientists are lazy writers. They use letters:
- v stands for velocitas (Latin for speed)
- s stands for spatium (distance/path)
- t stands for tempus (time)

5. Diagrams: Making pictures out of numbers

Lists with hundreds of numbers are terribly boring and hard to understand. That's why physicists draw diagrams (graphs)! In a diagram there are two axes (lines): The x-axis usually lies at the bottom on the ground (horizontal), the y-axis stands like a tree trunk upwards (vertical). A diagram shows us a process as a curve – you can see immediately what is happening!
